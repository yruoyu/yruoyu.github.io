Publications

Physics is an experimental science, so I like to keep one foot in theory and one on real hardware — not an armchair strategist. Please find more details on Google Scholar.

Theoretical work

When does a randomly evolving, repeatedly measured quantum particle first get detected — or first return home? These papers build the theory of that question and the surprising role of restarting a search.

Large fluctuations of the first detected quantum return time ★ Editors' Suggestion
R. Yin, K. Ziegler, F. Thiel, E. Barkai · Phys. Rev. Research 1, 033086 (2019)

If you keep asking a quantum particle "are you back where you started?", the time it first says yes can swing wildly from run to run. We show these fluctuations are not noise — they carry a topological fingerprint that jumps abruptly as the measurement rate is tuned.

Quantum walks: the mean first detected transition time
Q. Liu, R. Yin, K. Ziegler, E. Barkai · Phys. Rev. Research 2, 033113 (2020)

Here we ask how long, on average, until a monitored quantum walker is first found somewhere other than its starting point. A clean general formula emerges, and the mean time can even diverge — a behaviour we make intuitive by mapping it onto classical charges sitting on a disk.

Restart expedites quantum walk hitting times
R. Yin, E. Barkai · Phys. Rev. Lett. 130, 050802 (2023)

Sometimes the fastest way to find something is to give up and start over. We show that restarting a quantum search removes "dark states" — configurations where the particle would otherwise never be found — and can dramatically speed up detection, a quantum take on the classical idea of resetting.

Quantum first detection of a quantum walker on a perturbed ring
Y. Wang, R. Yin, et al. · Phys. Rev. Research 5, 013202 (2023)

Real systems are never perfect rings. We study how a single defect on the ring changes when — and whether — a quantum walker is detected, bridging the idealised theory and the imperfections of actual devices.

Instability in the quantum restart problem
R. Yin, Q. Wang, E. Barkai · Phys. Rev. E 109, 064150 (2024)

Restart helps, but not always smoothly. We found that the best moment to restart can behave erratically — tiny changes in the parameters cause sudden jumps in the optimal strategy, an instability with no classical counterpart.

Experimental work

Theory is only half the story. These papers take the ideas onto real IBM quantum computers, where finite measurements, noise, and hardware reality put the predictions to the test.

Restart uncertainty relation for monitored quantum dynamics
R. Yin, Q. Wang, S. Tornow, E. Barkai · Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. U.S.A. 122 (1), e2402912121 (2025)

We derived a new time–energy uncertainty relation for repeatedly measured quantum systems — and then tested it on IBM quantum processors. Theory and hardware agreed, showing that the predicted "broadening" of sharp transitions is a real, observable effect rather than a paper curiosity.

First hitting times on a quantum computer: tracking vs. local monitoring, topological effects, and dark states
Q. Wang, S. Ren, R. Yin, K. Ziegler, E. Barkai, S. Tornow · Entropy 26, 869 (2024)

We measured first-hitting times directly on an IBM quantum computer. The experiment confirmed the predicted quantization, exposed the dark states that hide a walker from detection, and showed how using only a finite number of measurements smooths out the otherwise sharp theoretical transitions.
